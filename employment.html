Employment

Breaking down state data on unemployment rates, total (nonfarm) payroll employment, and government employment.
Roll over the bar charts, scatter plots, maps, and legends to see additional data.

The national unemployment rate was 5.1 percent in September 2015. West Virginia’s unemployment rate (7.3 percent) remained the highest of any state. It was down 0.3 percentage points from August but was still up 1.0 points from September 2014. The next-highest unemployment rates were in New Mexico (6.8 percent), the District of Columbia (DC) and Nevada (both 6.7 percent). Four other states had September unemployment rates greater than 6.0 percent: Alaska (6.4 percent), Arizona (6.3 percent), Oregon (6.2 percent), and Mississippi (6.1 percent). The lowest unemployment rates were in North Dakota (2.8 percent) and Nebraska (2.9 percent). Another seven states had an unemployment rates below 4.0 percent: Hawaii and New Hampshire (3.4 percent), South Dakota (3.5 percent), Iowa and Utah (both 3.6 percent), Vermont (3.7 percent), and Minnesota (3.8 percent).

The national unemployment rate fell 0.8 percentage points between September 2014 and September 2015. West Virginia’s 1.0 percentage point increase was the greatest of any state. Six other states had unemployment rates increases over the year: New Mexico (0.5 points), Oklahoma and South Dakota (both 0.2 points), and Kansas, Minnesota, and North Dakota (all 0.1 points). Among these six states, only New Mexico’s September unemployment rate was above 5.0 percent. The unemployment rate in both Kansas and Oklahoma was 4.4 percent and the other three states had rates below 4.0 percent. The unemployment rate decreased the most over the year in Rhode Island (-1.8 points) and Michigan (-1.7 points). In total, 12 states and DC had unemployment rates drop 1.0 point or more between September 2014 and September 2015. However, seven of these states and DC still had September unemployment rates above 5.0 percent: DC (6.7 percent), Mississippi (6.1 percent), California (5.9 percent), Georgia (5.8 percent), Illinois and Rhode Island (5.4 percent), and Connecticut and Washington (both 5.2 percent)

Total national (public and private) nonfarm payroll employment increased 2.0 percent from September 2014 to September 2015. Total employment increased in all but four states: North Dakota (-1.6 percent), West Virginia (-1.5 percent), Wyoming (-0.9 percent), and Alaska (-0.1 percent). North Dakota’s and Wyoming’s biggest job losses were in the trade, transportation, and utilities sectors while West Virginia’s biggest losses were in construction. The largest increase in employment was in Utah (3.7 percent), which had job growth in all sectors. Employment grew 3.0 percent or more over the past 12 months in four additional states: South Carolina (3.2 percent), Idaho and Washington (both 3.1 percent), and Florida (3.0 percent).

Total public-sector employment increased 0.7 percent from September 2014 to September 2015. The largest decrease in government employment over the year was in New Hampshire (-4.2 percent), which lost both state and local government jobs. Another five states had public employment decline more than 1.0 percent: Louisiana (-1.4 percent), Montana (-1.3 percent), Minnesota (-1.2 percent), and Alaska and Hawaii (both -1.1 percent). Alaska was the only one of these states that lost both public-sector and total jobs. Three states had increases in public employment greater than 2.0 percent: Idaho (2.7 percent) and Oregon and Washington (both 2.4 percent). All three states also had total employment increase 2.8 percent or more. In total, 32 states and DC had increases in public jobs over the year. Only two of these states had decreases in total employment: North Dakota and Wyoming.
